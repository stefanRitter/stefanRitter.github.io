Online Romance: Taking it to the Next Level

Curated by Jenna Wortham June 22, 2015 0

You’ve done Match.com and Tinder, meetups and speed dating, sites and apps, social messaging and texts. About a quarter of online daters have found true romance – even love and marriage – on the digital superhighway. So what’s next? Where will algorithms and smartphones take cyber dating? Here’s a vision of the future – with an insightful look to the past – in articles and film.

Our collection:

Call Him Old-Fashioned

His parents married after a half-hour meeting. He can’t even choose where to have dinner in that amount of time. Still, the comedian Aziz Ansari says romance today is not much different from his parents’ day. We’re all looking for the same things, he writes in Time Magazine.

About those parents…

My parents had an arranged marriage. This always fascinated me. I am perpetually indecisive about even the most mundane things, and I couldn’t imagine navigating such a huge life decision so quickly.

But, really, what’s so different?

Today’s generations are looking (exhaustively) for soul mates, whether we decide to hit the altar or not, and we have more opportunities than ever to find them. The biggest changes have been brought by the $2.4 billion online-­dating industry, which has exploded in the past few years with the arrival of dozens of mobile apps. Throw in the fact that people now get married later in life than ever before, turning their early 20s into a relentless hunt for more romantic options than previous generations could have ever imagined, and you have a recipe for romance gone haywire. In the course of our research, I also discovered something surprising: the winding road from the classified section of yore to Tinder has taken an unexpected turn. Our phones and texts and apps might just be bringing us full circle, back to an old-fashioned version of courting that is closer to what my own parents experienced than you might guess.

In his book, "Modern Romance," written with the sociologist Eric Klineberg, Ansari looks at the way communication has changed. In this video, he and Jimmy Fallon discuss deciphering text messages.

India’s Modern Matchmaker

“Imagine eHarmony if it cut to the chase,” writes P. Nash Jenkins in a profile in The Atlantic about Shaadi.com, India’s most popular matrimonial website.

Bikas Das/Associated Press

When Indian entrepreneur Anupam Mittal created Shaadi.com in 1996, all he was really doing was modernizing a millennia-old operation, rendering the professional marriage broker almost obsolete in urban areas and posing competition to Indian newspapers, which have long turned a profit on matrimonial ads in their classified pages.

The difference with most western dating sites is that the final objective is not a casual encounter: it's marriage.

Imagine eHarmony if it cut to the chase. Unlike online dating services, which at least superficially foster some sort of romantic connection, and which are effectively nonexistent in India, matrimonial websites are predicated on the idea that the first meeting between two paired users will be to chat about their wedding. They succeed for the same reason every online resource does: They offer convenience and expediency in an arena with high demand for it. It’s connubial bliss for a 21st-century India, where, by some estimates, 90 percent of marriages still classify as “arranged”—in other words, established on factors other than mutual love and attraction between the bride and groom.

Breaking the Ice

How to start a conversation on Tinder, the so-called casual dating app? Sam Biddle of Gawker finds three little words that do the trick.

"There she is" does that perfectly. It's just cheesy enough to break the ice without scaring the object of your affection away. It gives her a wide range choices in response. And best of all, The Line is a goofy wink at the absurdity of swiping through Tinder's vast library of human beings. It's perfect enough—short, to the point, not too boring, not too gross, does not feature the word "pussy"—that I bet it would work not just for straight men but for people of all genders and sexualities. As long as you're okay with feeling just a little bit gross.
